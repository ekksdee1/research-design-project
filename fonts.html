Fonts in Typography

Typography is made up of 4 main types of fonts:

  • Serif
  • Sans-serif
  • Script
  • Decorative

Serif and sans-serif are the most commonly used fonts, often used in general paragraphs and typing. For example, Calibri is the default font for Microsoft Word.

The quick brown fox jumped over the fence, scurrying away.

Times New Roman is also another common font, used in academic papers, newspapers, etc.

The quick brown fox jumped over the fence, scurrying away.

Did you notice anything different about the fonts, specifically how the ends of the letters look? This is the main difference between serif and sans-serif fonts, the ends of the letters. Serif literally means "a slight projection finishing off a stroke of a letter", and sans means "without".

In serif fonts, the ends of the letters are more decorative and sharp, where as in sans-serif fonts, the letters are very simple and clean. In this case, the serif font is Times New Roman, and the sans-serif font is Calibri.

Next, let's move on to decorative and script fonts. Script fonts are quite simple, they generally look like a person's handwriting in cursive.

The quick brown fox jumped over the fence, scurrying away.

This is not to say that script fonts can only look like cursive, some script fonts are more formal.
